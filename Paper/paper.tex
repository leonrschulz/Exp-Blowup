\section{Background}
\label{sec_background}

Formulas of propositional logic are constructed from variables
(or \emph{atoms}) with the logical connectives negation $\neg$,
disjunction $\lor$, conjunction $\land$ and implication $\to$.

A \emph{literal} is a variable $x$ oder a negated variable
$\neg x$. The literals $x$ and $\neg x$ are \emph{complements}
of each other.
%The complement of a lieral $a$ is denoted by $\bar{a}$.

A formula $F$ is in \emph{negation normal form} (NNF), if $F$ contains
no implications, and negations in $F$ only occur immediately in front
of variables, i.e., $F$ is built from literals by conjunction and
disjunction only.

For a formula $F$ in NNF we define the dual formula $\bar F$ in NNF
by replacing every literal by its complement, and interchanging the
symbols $\land$ and $\lor$.
For every formula $F$ in NNF, $\bar{F}$ is equivalent to $\neg F$.

\begin{definition}
  We define the following classes of formulas:
\begin{itemize}
\item A \emph{term} is a conjunction $a_1 \lor \ldots \lor a_k$
  of literals.
\item A \emph{clause} is a disjunction $a_1 \lor \ldots \lor a_k$ von Literalen.
\item A formula in \emph{disjunctive normal form} (DNF) is a
  disjunction $T_1 \lor \ldots \lor T_m$ of terms.
\item A formula in \emph{conjunctive normal form} (CNF) is a
  conjunction $C_1 \land \ldots \land C_m$ of clauses.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Note that every formula in DNF or CNF is also in NNF.

Every formula can be transformed into an equivalent formula in DNF,
and in DNF, but the cost of this transformation is an exponential
increase in size in the worst case.

\begin{theorem} \label{dnfblowup}
  For all sufficiently large $n\in\Nat$, there are formulas $F_n$ in
  CNF with $|F_n| = O(n)$, such that every equivalent formula $G_n$ in
  DNF is of size $|G_n| \geq n2^n$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The formulas $F_n$ are defined as $F_n = \bigwedge_{i=1}^n ( x_{i,0} \lor x_{i,1} ) \land (\neg
x_{i,0} \lor \neg x_{i,1})$.

If $G_n = \bigvee_{j\leq m} T_j$ is a formula in CNF equivalent to $F_n$, then the following two claims hold, which together prove the statement.

Claim 1. Each of the terms $T_j$ contains exactly one of the literals $x_{i,0}$ and $x_{i,1}$, for every $i\leq n$. In particular, every term $T_j$ is of size at least $n$.

Claim 2. For every vector
$\epsilon = (\epsilon_1 , \ldots ,\epsilon_n) \in \{ 0,1 \}^n$,
there is one of the terms $T_j$ in  $G_n$, which contains the literals
$x_{1,\epsilon_1}, \ldots , x_{n,\epsilon_n}$.
In particular, $G_n$ has at least $2^n$ many terms.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary} \label{cnfblowup}
  For all sufficiently large $n\in\Nat$, there are formulas $F'_n$ in
  DNF with $|F'_n| = O(n)$, such that every equivalent formula $G_n$
  in CNF is of size $|G_n| \geq n2^n$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
  Let $F'_n := {\bar F}_n$ for the formulas $F_n$ of Theorem~\ref{dnfblowup}.
  If there were formulas  $G_n \equiv F'_n$ in CNF with $G_n < n2^n$,
  then ${\bar G}_n \equiv F_n$ would be a formula in DNF with
  $|{\bar G}_n| = |G_n| < n2^n$, contradicting Theorem~\ref{dnfblowup}.
\end{proof}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec_preliminaries}

A \emph{formula} is inductively defined as either an atom, falsehood ($\bot$), the negation of a formula, or the disjunction, conjunction or implication of two formulas.
A \emph{literal} is a special case of a formula consisting of either a positive or negative atom, or a positive or negative $\bot$.
%TODO: introduce notation x_{i,b} for i :: nat and b :: bool
%TODO: rephrase to use the notation instead of the type var
All atoms are of the type \textsf{var}, representing a combination of a Boolean value and a natural number.
A formula is \emph{conjunctive} if it is either a literal or its first subformula is a literal and its second a conjunctive formula.
Analogously a formula is \emph{disjunctive} if it is either a literal or its first subformula is a literal and its second a disjunctive formula.

A \emph{valuation} is a function from atoms to Boolean values.
The \emph{evaluation} of a formula w.r.t.\ a valuation is the Boolean value we get after replacing all atoms with the specific truth values assigned by the valuation.
We say that a valuation $\alpha$ \emph{satisfies} a formula $\phi$, written $\alpha \models \phi$, if the formula evaluates to true w.r.t.\ the valuation.
A formula $\phi$ is
\emph{satisfiable} if there exists a valuation that satisfies it (i.e., $\existsOne{\alpha}{\alpha \models \phi}$) and
\emph{tautological} if it is satisfied by every valuation (i.e., $\forallOne{\alpha}{\alpha \models \phi}$).
Two formulas $\phi$ and $\psi$ are \emph{equivalent} if they have the same truth value for every valuation (i.e., $\forallOne{\alpha}{\alpha \models \phi \iff \alpha \models \psi}$).

\begin{definitionx}
  \label{def_dualize}
  %TODO: tune spacing around type parameters here.
  The function $\tyAnnot{\cst{dualize}}{\tyFormula{\yA} \Rightarrow \tyFormula{\yA}}$ converts a formula in negation normal form to its dualized form by replacing each literal with its complementary:
  an atom $A$ becomes $\neg A$,
  $\bot$ becomes $\neg\bot$,
  a negation $\neg \phi$ becomes $\phi$,
  $\phi \lor \psi$ becomes $\isaAp{\cst{dualize}}{\phi} \land \isaAp{\cst{dualize}}{\psi}$, and
  $\phi \land \psi$ becomes $\isaAp{\cst{dualize}}{\phi} \lor \isaAp{\cst{dualize}}{\psi}$.
\end{definitionx}


\section{CNF to DNF}
\label{sec_CNF_to_DNF}

\begin{lemma}
  \label{aux_lemma_prop4_step1}
  Let $\phi$ be a conjunctive Formula. Let $\alpha$ be a valuation that satisfies $\phi$ and let $\beta$ be another valuation. If both valuations map all variables occuring in $\phi$ to the same Boolean value, then $\beta$ also satisfies $\phi$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}
  \label{aux_lemma_prop4_step3}
  Let $\phi$ be a conjunctive Formula, and let $\alpha$ be a valuation. If there exists a variable that occurs positively in $\phi$ and is mapped to false by $\alpha$, then $\alpha$ does not satisfy $\phi$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[TODO: Find Nice name (e.g., Weak ...)]
  \label{thm_cnf_to_dnf_blowup}
  Let $n > 0$ be a natural number.
  Let $Ts$ be a list of formulas.
  Let $G = \bigvee_{i = 0}^{\listLength{Ts}} \listNth{Ts}{i}$.
  If all formulas in $Ts$ are conjunctive and satisfiable, and
  if $F_n$ is equivalent to $G$,
  then $\isaAp{\cstSizef}{G} \ge n \cdot 2^n$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  %TODO LRS: search for interesting lemmas to be use here (e.g., Step n: We show stuff using \cref{thm_boring_lemma}.)
  We proceed with five steps:
  \textbf{(1)}~First, we show that every conjunctive formula $T \in \isaAp{\cst{set}}{Ts}$ contains for all $i \in \{1,...,n\}$ either the variable $x_{i,0}$ or $x_{i,1}$ positively.
  We prove this step by contradiction, deriving false in the two cases where either both variables $x_{i,0}$ or $x_{i,1}$ are positively contained or both are not positively contained in $T$.
  In both cases, we proceed by defining a specific valuation that satisfies $G_n$ but not $F_n$.
  In the second case, for instance, we modify the valuation that satisfies $T$ by changing the assignment of the variables $x_{i,0}$ and $x_{i,1}$ to false.
  For this modified valuation we then show, using \cref{aux_lemma_prop4_step1}, that it still satisfies $T$ and thus $G$. But also it does not satisfy $F_n$, due to the assignment of $x_{i,0}$ and $x_{i,1}$.
  \textbf{(2)}~From this, we derive that every $T \in \isaAp{\cst{set}}{Ts}$ has $\isaAp{\cstSizef}{T} \ge n$.
  \textbf{(3)}~Furthermore, we prove using step 1 that for every Boolean list $\epsilon = [\epsilon_1,...,\epsilon_n]$ there exists a $T \in \isaAp{\cst{set}}{Ts}$ that contains the corresponding variables $x_{1,\epsilon_1},...,x_{n,\epsilon_n}$ positively.
  If this is not the case for a specific list $\epsilon$, we show, using step 1, that every $T$ must contain one of the variables $x_{1,(\epsilon_1-1)},...,x_{n,(\epsilon_n-1)}$. We then define a concrete valuation that maps all variables $x_{i,\epsilon_i}$ to true and $x_{i,(\epsilon_1-i)}$ to false for $i = 1,...,n$. 
  Using this valuation, we now derive a contradiction, showing that it satisfies $F_n$ but not $G_n$, which follows from \cref{aux_lemma_prop4_step3}.
  \textbf{(4)}~From this, step 1, and the fact that there are $2^n$ Boolean lists of length $n$, we derive that $\listLength{Ts} \ge 2^n$.
  \textbf{(5)}~Finally, we show that $\isaAp{\cstSizef}{G} \ge n \cdot 2^n$ by combining the results from steps 2 and 4.
\end{proof}
